Dr. Sripati Jana

Postdoctoral Researcher · EPFL - Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Lausanne

I am a research scientist working on the development of sustainable catalysis and organic synthesis. My work centers on designing chiral catalysts and ligands that make difficult asymmetric transformations possible, giving access to highly enantioenriched molecules — compounds that play a vital role across natural products, pharmaceuticals, and many other domains. I aim to do this in ways that are efficient, selective, and mindful of how chemistry can be made greener. A particular focus of mine is single-electron-transfer-mediated asymmetric catalysis built on chiral carbene catalysts. I’m drawn to reactions that are hard to control, and to understanding why they behave the way they do: most of my projects pair hands-on experiment with computational study (DFT), so that reaction design is guided by mechanism rather than trial and error.

I currently pursue this work as a postdoctoral researcher at EPFL, in the Laboratory of Asymmetric Catalysis and Synthesis. Before that, I completed my Ph.D. at RWTH Aachen University, where I developed new methodologies — spanning transition-metal catalysis, photochemistry, and metallaphotoredox strategies — for constructing complex carbon–carbon and carbon–heteroatom bonds. During that time I also spent a research visit at the Polish Academy of Sciences in Warsaw, using EPR spectroscopy to probe triplet intermediates in photocatalyzed reactions. Earlier, I earned my M.Sc. in Chemistry from IIT Kharagpur and my B.Sc. (Honours) from Ramakrishna Mission Vivekananda Centenary College, Rahara — the start of a path that has since taken me from West Bengal, India, through Germany, to Switzerland.

Across these chapters I’ve authored over 40 publications in leading journals, including the Journal of the American Chemical Society, Nature Communications, Angewandte Chemie International Edition, ACS Catalysis, Chemical Science, and Chem Catalysis. I’ve also come to value the parts of research that happen between people as much as the chemistry itself: mentoring students, leading projects from first idea to finished paper, and building collaborations where good ideas travel freely.
